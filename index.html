About Me

I am a geospatial scientist with expertise in remote sensing and geospatial data science. I am currently a postdoctoral research associate at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, and I have previously held postdoctoral positions at the University of Arizona and Boise State University.

My research focuses on using remote sensing and geospatial data science to study climate change and its impacts on nature and society.

What I Do

Geospatial & Environmental Data Science
I use machine learning to interpret and predict from big geospatial datasets — investigating land use and land cover change, assessing the impacts of disturbances, and predicting land surface variables from remote sensing data. I primarily use Python, but I am also proficient in R.

Earth System Modeling
I work with large process-based climate and Earth system models including CLM, ED-V2, WRF, DART (data assimilation), and GCAM. My interest lies in integrating these models with remote sensing observations, eddy covariance data, field data, and ecological theory to study the carbon cycle, land surface radiation budget, and land use/land cover change.

Theoretical & Applied Remote Sensing
I use 1-D and 3-D radiative transfer modeling to simulate light interaction with land surface materials, and numerical inversion techniques to estimate land surface properties. I have extensive experience with optical (hyperspectral/multispectral), lidar, and solar-induced fluorescence data from ground-based, airborne, and satellite platforms.
